\begin{abstract}
Symbolic regression (SR) discovers closed-form mathematical equations from
data—enabling human-interpretable models in science and medicine.  Despite
rapid progress, three persistent challenges limit its applicability in
real-world settings: (i) \emph{high-dimensional feature spaces} cause search
inefficiency as the algorithm explores irrelevant variables; (ii)
\emph{formula selection} from the Pareto front of accuracy–complexity
trade-offs remains largely heuristic; and (iii) \emph{data irregularities}
such as severe inter-correlation, class imbalance, and missingness degrade
symbolic search quality.  We introduce \textbf{DeepPySR}, an enhanced SR
framework built on SymbolicRegression.jl, with three novel contributions.
\textbf{First}, we propose a \emph{dynamic variable pruning schedule} (DVPS)
that linearly ramps up mutation pressure to eliminate uninformative variables
during the evolutionary search, substantially reducing the search space and
preventing overfitting through spurious features.
\textbf{Second}, we introduce an \emph{exponential Pareto selection criterion}
(EPS) that jointly optimises R$^2$ and equation complexity via a principled,
user-tunable score function, replacing ad-hoc thresholding.
\textbf{Third}, we extend the framework to \emph{multi-layer symbolic
regression} (DeepSR), where intermediate latent functions are automatically
discovered and composed hierarchically, reducing the burden imposed by
inter-correlated predictors.
We evaluate DeepPySR on five Feynman physics benchmarks and seven real-world
datasets spanning biomedicine and education.  DeepPySR recovers exact physics
equations (R$^2\!\approx\!1.0$) from noise-free data and outperforms both
PySR and all black-box baselines (XGBoost, Random Forest, MLP, KAN) on body
fat (R$^2$: 0.794 vs.\ 0.702), heart disease (F1: 0.898 vs.\ 0.787), and
student performance prediction (R$^2$: 0.964 vs.\ 0.948) while returning
interpretable analytical formulas that connect to domain knowledge.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The tension between predictive accuracy and interpretability is one of the
central problems in applied machine learning.  In scientific disciplines—from
biomedicine to astrophysics—understanding \emph{why} a model makes a
prediction is often as important as the prediction itself.  A cardiologist
needs not just a risk score but the mechanistic factors driving it; an
epidemiologist studying childhood BMI trajectories needs an equation that can
be communicated and acted on.

Real-world biomedical and social-science datasets impose additional
difficulties beyond prediction accuracy.  First, \emph{inter-correlated
covariates} (ICC) are ubiquitous: weight, waist circumference, BMI, glucose,
and insulin are mutually predictive, making it difficult to isolate a single
dominant predictor or to build a coherent multivariate formula.  Second,
\emph{class imbalance} is the rule rather than the exception—stroke events
affect fewer than 5\% of participants in population studies, and rare adverse
outcomes may appear in fewer than 1\% of records.  Standard responses to these
problems—selecting a subset of features via variable importance ranking, or
synthetically augmenting the minority class with SMOTE-like
methods~\citep{chawla2002smote}—are workarounds that add complexity and may
introduce artefacts.  A modelling framework that tolerates imbalance and
identifies the most informative features automatically would be preferable.

Symbolic regression (SR) addresses the interpretability need by searching for
analytical mathematical expressions that fit observed
data~\citep{koza1994genetic}.  Unlike black-box models, SR outputs
human-readable equations that can be inspected, simplified, and connected to
physical laws.  The field has seen renewed interest following the release of
PySR~\citep{cranmer2023interpretable} and the Feynman SR
benchmark~\citep{udrescu2020ai}, with recent methods achieving exact recovery
of fundamental physics laws from noisy measurements.

Kolmogorov-Arnold Networks (KAN)~\citep{liu2024kan} have been proposed as a
differentiable, architecture-driven path to interpretability: learnable
B-spline functions on network edges enable symbolic simplification after
training.  In principle, KANs offer a fast, gradient-based alternative to
evolutionary SR.  In practice, however, we show that KAN's symbolic extraction
step fails systematically on standard benchmarks—producing degenerate or
high-error formulas—while the numeric KAN performs comparably to conventional
neural networks.  KANs also require smooth, continuous variable relationships
and are sensitive to scale, limiting their applicability to the messy
data encountered in biomedicine.

\subsection{Limitations of existing approaches}
\label{sec:limits}

\textbf{AI Feynman and clean-data assumption.}  AIFeynman~\citep{udrescu2020ai}
achieves strong results on the Feynman benchmark by exploiting physics-specific
heuristics (dimensional analysis, modular network detection).  Its methodology,
however, is designed for ideally clean, noiseless data with well-defined
physical dimensions—assumptions that rarely hold in biomedical or social-science
settings where variables are measured with error, units are heterogeneous, and
functional relationships are not derivable from first principles.

\textbf{KAN limitations.}  KANs~\citep{liu2024kan} are differentiable and
fast to train, but their symbolic extraction step requires the network to
learn activation functions that precisely match a library of elementary
functions (sin, exp, $\sqrt{\cdot}$, etc.).  This places a high burden on
optimisation and network width.  Furthermore, KANs assume variable continuity
and respond poorly to discrete or ordinal inputs, binary features, and large
correlations among inputs—all common in clinical datasets.

\textbf{General SR challenges with real-world data.}
Despite PySR's tolerance for noise and mixed data types compared to earlier SR
methods, three further challenges remain:

\textbf{(1) Curse of dimensionality.}  Genetic programming (GP) explores an
exponentially large tree space.  When input dimensionality grows, the search
algorithm spends excessive effort combining irrelevant features, slowing
convergence to the true expression.  Existing methods handle this through
manual feature pre-selection or static parsimony penalties, neither of which
adapts to the evolving population.

\textbf{(2) Inter-correlation and ICC.}  For highly intercorrelated variables—
such as anthropometric measurements (weight, BMI, waist circumference) or
clinical markers (fasting glucose, HbA1c, insulin)—standard SR may return
arbitrary combinations of equivalent predictors rather than a principled,
parsimonious formula.  The search may ``collapse'' onto a cluster of
correlated variables, saturating complexity without improving generalisation.

\textbf{(3) Complexity control and formula selection.}  SR returns a Pareto
front trading accuracy against equation complexity.  Adaptive parsimony
scaling~\citep{cranmer2023interpretable} mitigates population collapse around
a single complexity level, but once a front is obtained, practitioners must
still choose among equations by hand or with simple threshold rules that
ignore both user accuracy requirements and domain constraints on simplicity.

\textbf{(4) Expressive limitations.}  Standard SR discovers a single
expression $y = f(\mathbf{x})$.  Many real-world systems are better described
by hierarchical compositions—a biomarker might itself be an unknown function
of measured variables that feeds into a higher-level outcome model.  Standard
SR cannot represent such latent intermediate structure without manual
engineering.

\subsection{Contributions}

We present \textbf{DeepPySR}, an extension of the SymbolicRegression.jl
engine~\citep{cranmer2023interpretable}, with the following contributions:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Dynamic Variable Pruning Schedule (DVPS).}  A scheduled
    mutation that gradually introduces a ``prune variable'' operator during
    evolutionary search.  The pruning weight follows a linear ramp from zero
    to a maximum multiplier, allowing early exploration and late feature
    elimination.  This is novel both algorithmically (embedded in the GP
    mutation weights) and practically (automatically performs feature
    selection without a separate pre-processing step).

  \item \textbf{Exponential Pareto Selection (EPS).}  A scoring criterion for
    selecting the single best equation from the Pareto front:
    \[
      \mathrm{score}(i) = \bigl[\max(10^{-4},\, R^2_i)\bigr]^{\rho}
                          \cdot \exp\!\bigl(-\lambda\,(c_i - 1)\bigr),
    \]
    where $R^2_i$ is predictive accuracy, $c_i$ is equation complexity,
    $\rho\!\in\!\{1.0, 1.5, 2.0\}$ is a user-tunable accuracy exponent, and
    $\lambda\!\in\!\{0.001, 0.005, 0.01\}$ is a user-tunable complexity
    penalty.  Both parameters are searched via cross-validated grid search,
    allowing users to explicitly favour accuracy or simplicity according to
    their application needs.

  \item \textbf{Multi-Layer Symbolic Regression (DeepSR).}  A hierarchical
    SR architecture where the system discovers latent intermediate functions
    and composes them layer by layer.  The final model takes the form
    $y = f(g_1(\mathbf{x}),\, g_2(\mathbf{x}),\, \ldots,\, g_K(\mathbf{x}))$,
    where each $g_k$ is itself a symbolic expression.  A key property is
    that the outer function $f$ typically selects one most-representative
    variable from each cluster of intercorrelated predictors, decomposing the
    ICC problem into a tree-like discovery process rather than a flat search
    over a correlated cluster.
\end{enumerate}

We demonstrate these contributions on five Feynman physics equations and seven
real-world datasets from biomedicine and education, including the RAINE
Generation 2 longitudinal cohort.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\textbf{Symbolic regression.}  Genetic programming~\citep{koza1994genetic}
is the classical approach to SR.  Modern implementations include
gplearn~\citep{stephens2015gplearn}, Operon~\citep{burlacu2020operon}, and
PySR/SymbolicRegression.jl~\citep{cranmer2023interpretable}.  PySR introduced
adaptive parsimony scaling and a multi-population evolutionary scheme that
significantly outperforms classical GP.  AIFeynman~\citep{udrescu2020ai}
incorporates physics-inspired heuristics (dimensional analysis, neural
networks for module detection) to recover Feynman equations exactly.
DSR~\citep{petersen2021deep} uses a recurrent neural network as a policy to
generate expression trees, trained by reinforcement learning.

\textbf{Feature selection in GP.}  Prior work has explored feature selection
as a pre-processing step~\citep{hein2018interpretable} or through
multi-objective evolution that penalises variable
usage~\citep{virgolin2020improving}.  Our DVPS differs by embedding feature
pruning directly as a scheduled mutation operator, requiring no separate
selection phase and adapting dynamically to the population state.

\textbf{Interpretable neural networks.}  KAN~\citep{liu2024kan} places
learnable B-spline functions on network edges, producing neural networks that
can be symbolically simplified.  EQL~\citep{sahoo2018learning} constrains
network activations to symbolic functions.  Both approaches require network
training, which can be expensive and may produce fragile symbolic extractions.

\textbf{Multi-layer SR.}  The idea of composing SR layers has appeared in
deep SR works~\citep{kim2020integration} and feature engineering pipelines,
but has not been systematically integrated into modern evolutionary SR
frameworks with automated latent variable discovery.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

\subsection{Symbolic Regression Preliminaries}
\label{sec:sr_prelim}

Let $\mathcal{D} = \{(\mathbf{x}_i, y_i)\}_{i=1}^N$ be a dataset with
$\mathbf{x}_i \in \mathbb{R}^d$ and $y_i \in \mathbb{R}$.  SR seeks an
expression tree $f$ drawn from a grammar $\mathcal{G}$ of operators
(e.g., $+, \times, \sin, \exp$) and operands (input variables or constants)
such that $f(\mathbf{x}) \approx y$.  The search space is combinatorially
large, so evolutionary algorithms evolve a population of candidate trees by
repeated mutation and selection.

We build on SymbolicRegression.jl (the Julia back-end of
PySR)~\citep{cranmer2023interpretable}, which runs multiple independent
evolutionary populations in parallel and periodically migrates the best
individuals across populations.  The cost of each expression combines
predictive loss $\mathcal{L}$ and a parsimony penalty proportional to
the number of nodes~$c$:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{cost}(f) = \mathcal{L}(f) + \beta \cdot c(f),
  \label{eq:base_cost}
\end{equation}
where $\beta$ is the parsimony coefficient.  After search, PySR returns a
Pareto front of non-dominated (loss, complexity) pairs.

\subsection{Dynamic Variable Pruning Schedule (DVPS)}
\label{sec:dvps}

\textbf{Motivation.}  In high-dimensional datasets ($d \gg 1$), expression
trees tend to include many irrelevant variables early in the search because
any variable can reduce loss slightly by overfitting.  Static parsimony
penalises tree size but does not specifically target variable diversity.  We
introduce a dedicated mutation operator, \texttt{prune\_variable}, that
replaces a leaf variable node with a numeric constant.  Its weight in the
mutation schedule grows linearly as the search matures.

\textbf{Schedule function.}  At iteration $t$, the effective weight of the
pruning mutation is:
\begin{equation}
  w_{\mathrm{prune}}(t) = w_0 \cdot \tau(t) \cdot M,
  \label{eq:prune_schedule}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
  \tau(t) = \min\!\left(1,\, \max\!\left(0,\,
            \frac{t - T_{\mathrm{start}}}{T_{\mathrm{ramp}}}\right)\right),
\end{equation}
where $w_0$ is the base pruning weight (default $1.0$), $T_{\mathrm{start}}$
is the iteration at which pruning begins, $T_{\mathrm{ramp}}$ is the ramp
duration over which the weight linearly increases from $0$ to full, and $M$ is
the maximum multiplier cap.  Before $T_{\mathrm{start}}$, $\tau(t) = 0$ and
no pruning occurs; after $T_{\mathrm{start}} + T_{\mathrm{ramp}}$, $\tau(t)
= 1$ and pruning is applied at full weight $w_0 \cdot M$.

This schedule has three desirable properties: (i) early iterations are
unaffected, allowing exploration of all features; (ii) pruning pressure ramps
smoothly, preventing sudden disruption of the population; (iii) a cap $M$
prevents pruning from overwhelming other mutation operators.

\textbf{Effect on search.}  In the early phase ($t < T_{\mathrm{start}}$),
the algorithm behaves identically to baseline PySR.  As the schedule activates,
trees containing irrelevant variables are preferentially replaced—either by
equivalent trees without that variable or by simpler alternatives—because the
fitness landscape increasingly rewards simplicity.  This is analogous to
L$_1$ regularisation applied to tree structure rather than weight magnitudes.

\textbf{Implementation.}  The schedule is implemented in
\texttt{Mutate.jl} of SymbolicRegression.jl.  At each mutation step, the
current iteration is queried and $w_{\mathrm{prune}}(t)$ is recomputed before
sampling the mutation type from the weight vector.

Algorithm~\ref{alg:dvps} summarises the full evolutionary loop with DVPS.

\begin{algorithm}
\caption{Evolutionary SR with Dynamic Variable Pruning Schedule}
\label{alg:dvps}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\State \textbf{Input:} dataset $\mathcal{D}$, operators $\mathcal{G}$,
       params $T_{\mathrm{start}}, T_{\mathrm{ramp}}, M, w_0$
\State Initialise population $\mathcal{P}$ of random expression trees
\For{$t = 1, \ldots, T_{\mathrm{max}}$}
  \State $\tau \gets \min(1, \max(0, (t - T_{\mathrm{start}})/T_{\mathrm{ramp}}))$
  \State $w_{\mathrm{prune}} \gets w_0 \cdot \tau \cdot M$
  \State Update mutation weights with $w_{\mathrm{prune}}$
  \ForAll{individuals $f \in \mathcal{P}$}
    \State Sample mutation type from updated weight distribution
    \If{mutation = \texttt{prune\_variable}}
      \State Replace a random variable node with a constant
    \Else
      \State Apply standard mutation (e.g., \texttt{mutate\_operator})
    \EndIf
    \State Evaluate $\mathrm{cost}(f)$ on $\mathcal{D}$
    \State Update Pareto front
  \EndFor
  \State Migrate top individuals across sub-populations
\EndFor
\State \textbf{Return:} Pareto front of (loss, complexity) pairs
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Exponential Pareto Selection (EPS)}
\label{sec:eps}

After evolutionary search, SR produces a Pareto front $\mathcal{F} =
\{f_1, f_2, \ldots\}$ of non-dominated solutions indexed by accuracy and
complexity.  Selecting the best single formula is non-trivial: purely
accuracy-based selection favours the most complex equation; purely
complexity-based selection discards high-performing models.  Importantly,
the standard Pareto score does not account for application-specific
requirements—a clinical formula must be simple enough to audit, while a
data-driven predictor might tolerate higher complexity.

We define the following scoring function over the Pareto front:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{score}(f_i) =
    \bigl[\max(10^{-4},\, R^2_i)\bigr]^{\rho}
    \cdot \exp\!\bigl(-\lambda\,(c_i - 1)\bigr),
  \label{eq:eps}
\end{equation}
where $R^2_i$ is the cross-validated R$^2$ of equation $f_i$, $c_i$ is its
node count (complexity), $\rho \geq 1$ is the accuracy exponent, and
$\lambda > 0$ is the complexity decay constant.  The selected formula is
$f^* = \arg\max_i \mathrm{score}(f_i)$.

\textbf{Interpretation.}  The first factor rewards accuracy superlinearly
(when $\rho > 1$), magnifying differences between good and mediocre formulas.
The exponential decay penalises complexity in a multiplicative rather than
additive way: each additional node reduces the score by a factor
$e^{-\lambda}$, so the penalty grows proportionally with complexity.
Compared to additive parsimony, exponential penalisation prevents very complex
equations from surviving even with marginally higher R$^2$.

\textbf{Hyperparameters and user control.}  We grid-search over
$\rho \in \{1.0, 1.5, 2.0\}$ and $\lambda \in \{0.001, 0.005, 0.01\}$
using cross-validated performance.  Larger $\rho$ favours accuracy;
larger $\lambda$ imposes stronger simplicity pressure.  Users can supply a
candidate list of $(\rho, \lambda)$ pairs tailored to their domain: a clinical
audit requiring formulas of fewer than 15 nodes might set $\lambda = 0.01$
and $\rho = 1.0$, while an automated prediction task might use $\lambda = 0.001$
and $\rho = 2.0$.  The floor $\max(10^{-4}, R^2_i)$ prevents numerical issues
when $R^2 \leq 0$.

\subsection{Multi-Layer Symbolic Regression (DeepSR)}
\label{sec:deepsr}

\textbf{Motivation.}  Many systems exhibit hierarchical functional structure.
For example, body fat may depend on a complex interaction of anthropometric
measurements that cannot be expressed as a single shallow expression tree.
Standard SR is limited to single-layer $y = f(\mathbf{x})$ relationships.
Additionally, in the presence of ICC, standard SR may converge to arbitrary
linear combinations of mutually predictive variables.  DeepSR addresses this
by treating a cluster of correlated variables as a subtree rather than a flat
cluster: the outer function selects one representative compound feature, and
inner layers reveal how that feature is constituted by the correlated
predictors.

\textbf{Architecture.}  DeepSR discovers the model:
\begin{equation}
  y = f\!\bigl(g_1(\mathbf{x}),\, g_2(\mathbf{x}),\, \ldots,\, g_K(\mathbf{x})\bigr),
  \label{eq:deep_sr}
\end{equation}
where $f$ and each $g_k$ are symbolic expressions, and $K$ is the number of
intermediate latent variables.  The key insight is that after discovering the
outer function $f$, the latent inputs $g_k$ can themselves be treated as SR
targets, enabling recursive decomposition.

\textbf{Algorithm.}  DeepSR uses a breadth-first queue:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Outer-layer SR.}  Run SR on the original target $y$ against
    features $\mathbf{x}$.  The selected expression $\hat{f}$ may reference
    raw features and implicitly defined latent variables.

  \item \textbf{Latent variable extraction.}  Identify subexpressions within
    $\hat{f}$ that could be independently discovered; treat them as new SR
    targets $\{g_k\}_{k=1}^K$.

  \item \textbf{Inner-layer SR.}  For each $g_k$, run SR with $\mathbf{x}$
    as features and the residual of $g_k$ as the target.  Discovered
    expressions $\hat{g}_k$ are substituted back into $\hat{f}$.

  \item \textbf{Repeat} until no latent variables remain or maximum depth
    is reached.
\end{enumerate}

Each SR call uses the full DVPS and EPS machinery from
Sections~\ref{sec:dvps}--\ref{sec:eps}.  The composed expression is evaluated
by recursively substituting $\hat{g}_k$ into $\hat{f}$ using SymPy for
symbolic manipulation and \texttt{lambdify} for efficient vectorised prediction.

\textbf{Visualisation.}  Figure~\ref{fig:circle} shows the DeepSR
relationship graph (circle plot) for two datasets.  Each node on the
circumference is a feature or the target; arcs connect variables that appear

\section{Datasets}
\label{sec:datasets}

We evaluate on two categories of benchmarks: synthetic physics equations and
real-world datasets.  The real-world datasets are chosen to span diverse
characteristics: (i) \emph{task type} (regression, binary classification);
(ii) \emph{data quality} (clean, moderate missingness, noisy labels);
(iii) \emph{variable structure} (independent features, ICC clusters);
(iv) \emph{class balance} (balanced, moderate imbalance, severe imbalance);
and (v) \emph{achievable accuracy} (datasets where black-box models score both
high and low).  Importantly, no feature engineering is applied to any dataset;
all models receive the raw (label-encoded or standardised) feature set.  This
design choice directly tests each model's ability to identify relevant features
without pre-processing guidance—a critical capability in exploratory science.

\subsection{Real-World Datasets}

\textbf{Body Fat} (252 samples, 14 features; regression).
The Johnson~\cite{johnson1996fitting} dataset measures body fat percentage alongside
13 anthropometric measurements (abdomen, wrist, weight, neck, hip, thigh,
etc.).  Anthropometric features are highly intercorrelated (ICC), making
feature selection non-trivial.  An established domain formula
(Durnin–Womersley approximation) is available for qualitative comparison.
Black-box models achieve moderate R$^2$ ($\leq\!0.70$) on this dataset.

\textbf{Heart Disease} (303 samples, 13 features; binary classification).
The Cleveland Heart Disease dataset~\citep{detrano1989international} contains
clinical measurements (cholesterol, chest pain type, electrocardiographic
results, maximum heart rate, etc.) and a binary target indicating coronary
artery disease presence.  Class imbalance is moderate (54\% positive).
Categorical and ordinal inputs mix with continuous ones.

\textbf{Wine Quality} (Red: 1,599 samples; White: 4,898 samples; 11
physicochemical features; regression).  The UCI Wine Quality
dataset~\citep{cortez2009modeling} provides quality scores (3–9) for
Portuguese Vinho Verde wines.  We treat quality as a regression target; red
and white wines are modelled separately due to differing chemical profiles.
This is a noisy-label dataset (quality is a subjective human rating) where all
models achieve moderate R$^2$, testing SR's performance under label noise.

\textbf{Stroke} (5,110 samples, 10 features; binary classification).
The Kaggle Stroke Prediction dataset provides medical history, lifestyle, and
demographic features with a highly imbalanced binary target (4.9\% positive).
This dataset tests model performance under severe class imbalance without
SMOTE augmentation—deliberately challenging SR to handle imbalance by
structure rather than resampling.

\textbf{Student Performance} (Math: 395 samples; Portuguese: 649 samples;
33 features; regression).  The UCI Student Performance dataset~\citep{cortez2008student}
records demographic, family, and school-related attributes of secondary-school
students in Portugal, along with first- (G1), second- (G2), and final-period
(G3) grades.  G3 is the target.  Features include parental employment, home
address, study time, extra-curricular support, internet access, alcohol
consumption, and health status.  The strong correlation between G1, G2, and
G3 (ICC) challenges standard SR, while the rich social and demographic context
tests whether SR can extract meaningful non-linear predictors beyond the
obvious grade progression.  This is a dataset where tree-ensemble models
achieve high R$^2$ ($\leq\!0.87$), setting a demanding comparison.

\textbf{BMI Trajectories—RAINE Generation 2 Cohort.}
The RAINE study is a prospective longitudinal cohort tracking participants
from birth in Western Australia.  We use Generation 2 data for a
\emph{longitudinal} prediction task: predicting adult BMI (age 27) from early
childhood measurements across multiple time points (ages 1–10).  Features
include anthropometric measures (weight, height, BMI z-score), polygenic
risk scores (PRS for BMI; SUM\_PGS002313, SUM\_PGS002853,
SUM\_PGS002161), and sociodemographic variables (parental education,
prenatal alcohol/smoking exposure, prepreg weight).  The dataset has
approximately 125 candidate features, moderate missingness, and significant
ICC among predictors—making it a demanding real-world discovery problem.

\textbf{Diabetes} (70,692 samples, 21 features; binary classification).
The CDC Behavioral Risk Factor Surveillance System (BRFSS) Diabetes Health
Indicators dataset~\citep{cdc2015brfss} contains self-reported health
behaviors, conditions, and demographics, with a binary diabetes/pre-diabetes
target.  Severe class imbalance and the subjective nature of self-reported
features make this a challenging task.  Due to a reduced iteration budget
(100 iterations vs.\ 500 for other datasets), results are reported in the
Supplementary Material.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\subsection{Test Pipeline}

All experiments use a standardised pipeline:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Preprocessing.}  Categorical variables are label-encoded;
    continuous features are standardised for baselines but passed raw to
    SR models (to aid formula interpretability).  No feature engineering
    or feature selection is applied before training.

  \item \textbf{Cross-validation.}  We use 5-fold cross-validation with
    stratified splits for classification tasks, stratified by a relevant
    covariate (e.g., G2 for student performance) for regression, and
    standard random splits otherwise.  Results are averaged across folds.
    For class-imbalanced datasets (stroke, diabetes), SMOTE oversampling
    is applied to baseline classifiers for a fair comparison; SR models
    are deliberately tested \emph{without} SMOTE to evaluate their
    native tolerance for imbalance.

  \item \textbf{SR hyperparameter search.}  We run a grid search over
    pruning parameters ($T_{\mathrm{start}} \in \{25, 50, 75\}$ iterations,
    $T_{\mathrm{ramp}} \in \{50, 100, 150\}$) and adaptive parsimony
    scaling ($\alpha \in \{1.0, 10.0, 50.0\}$), giving 27 SR configurations
    per dataset.  Pareto selection hyperparameters ($\rho \in \{1.0, 1.5,
    2.0\}$, $\lambda \in \{0.001, 0.005, 0.01\}$) are searched independently
    over the resulting Pareto fronts.

  \item \textbf{Formula evaluation.}  For each configuration, the best
    formula is selected using EPS (Eq.~\ref{eq:eps}) and evaluated on the
    held-out fold.  An ``interpretable'' formula is additionally defined as
    the best-scoring formula with complexity $\leq 40$ nodes.

  \item \textbf{Post-hoc analyses.}  Following cross-validated training we
    conduct three additional analyses on the best-performing configuration:
    (a) \emph{deep analysis}—training DeepSR on the full dataset and
    visualising the discovered hierarchical formula as a relationship graph;
    (b) \emph{statistical analysis}—computing Wilcoxon signed-rank tests
    comparing DeepPySR and PySR performance distributions across folds; and
    (c) \emph{convergence analysis}—tracking best-formula R$^2$ or F1 per
    iteration for DeepPySR vs.\ PySR on the Feynman and BMI datasets.

  \item \textbf{Reporting.}  We report the best result over the
    hyperparameter grid.  For baselines, we report the best result over
    standard hyperparameter tuning.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Baselines}

We compare against:

\textbf{Black-box models:} XGBoost~\citep{chen2016xgboost}, Random
Forest~\citep{breiman2001random}, Extra Trees~\citep{geurts2006extremely},
Logistic/Linear Regression (ElasticNet for regression,
LogisticRegression for classification), and a Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP)
with two hidden layers (128, 64 units, ReLU activation, Adam optimiser).

\textbf{Interpretable neural network:} KAN~\citep{liu2024kan} (trained with
default settings) and KANSym, its symbolically-extracted version using the
symbolic regression module in the KAN library.

\textbf{Symbolic regression baseline:} PySR (vanilla, without DVPS), run
with the same operator set and iteration budget.

\subsection{SR Configuration}

All SR runs use:
binary operators $\{+, -, \times, \div, \mathrm{cond}\}$ and
unary operators $\{\exp, \log, \sin, \sqrt{\,\cdot\,}\}$;
maximum tree size 40;
100 populations of 200 individuals;
200 evolution cycles per iteration;
500 iterations (100 for stroke and diabetes due to computational budget);
parsimony coefficient $\beta = 0.001$;
pruning maximum multiplier $M = 0.7$.

\subsection{Computational Resources}

Experiments were conducted on the Setonix supercomputer at the Pawsey
Supercomputing Research Centre and on the ICRAR computing cluster.  Each SR
configuration was run as an independent parallel job.  The 27-configuration
grid requires approximately 1,200–2,500 CPU-hours per dataset (depending on
iteration budget and dataset size).

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

For regression: coefficient of determination (R$^2$), root mean squared error
(RMSE), and mean absolute error (MAE).
For classification: accuracy, precision, recall, F1 score, and AUC-ROC.
Formula complexity is reported as the number of nodes in the expression tree.

\section{Analysis}
\label{sec:analysis}

\subsection{Effect of Dynamic Variable Pruning}

In the body fat dataset (14 features), the best interpretable DeepPySR formula
uses 4 variables (abdomen, wrist, weight, neck/ankle), while PySR's best
formula at comparable complexity uses 3 variables but achieves lower R$^2$
(0.702 vs.\ 0.776 at 40 nodes).  DVPS allows the search to maintain richer
variable coverage early—leading to discovery of the neck correction term—while
pruning unrelated measurements (chest, bicep, forearm) that standard PySR
may include as overfitting artefacts.

Across the full hyperparameter grid, configurations with DVPS (any
$T_{\mathrm{start}} > 0$) outperform the no-pruning baseline (PySR) in 6 out
of 7 real-world datasets.  For the Feynman equations, DVPS has negligible
effect on low-dimensional inputs ($n_f \leq 4$) but improves search efficiency
measurably for I.9.18 ($n_f = 7$) and BMI longitudinal ($n_f \approx 125$).

\subsection{Pareto Selection Criterion}

The EPS criterion (Eq.~\ref{eq:eps}) consistently selects shorter, more
generalisable formulas than naive accuracy maximisation from the Pareto front.
Across all datasets and folds, EPS-selected formulas achieve better held-out
R$^2$ than the most accurate formula on the Pareto front in the majority of
fold/dataset combinations, with an average complexity reduction of 12--18
nodes.

The exponential penalty is critical: with linear parsimony (removing the
exponent), large penalties are required to select compact formulas, and the
result is sensitive to the scale of $\lambda$.  The exponential formulation
maintains sensitivity across complexity scales, preventing very complex
equations from surviving even with marginally higher R$^2$.

For the student performance dataset, the best formula (R$^2 = 0.964$,
complexity 39) is selected at $\lambda = 0.001$ and $\rho = 1.0$, reflecting
that the accuracy gain from using G2 as a dominant predictor justifies the
complexity.  Increasing $\lambda$ to 0.01 selects a simpler 11-node formula
(R$^2 \approx 0.94$) that relies only on G2 and absences.

\subsection{Accuracy–Interpretability Trade-off}

A core strength of SR is the explicit Pareto front.  For body fat, the
interpretable variant (complexity $\leq 41$ nodes, R$^2 = 0.776$) achieves
97.7\% of the accuracy of the best formula (R$^2 = 0.794$)—a modest trade-off
for a substantial gain in readability.  For BMI longitudinal, the best formula
already has only 11 nodes and is fully interpretable.

The Pareto fronts for DeepPySR generally dominate those of PySR at
equivalent complexity on real-world datasets: for body fat, DeepPySR achieves
$\Delta R^2 \approx +0.05\text{--}0.09$ at each complexity level from
15 to 50 nodes.  This dominance is attributable to DVPS eliminating irrelevant
variables that consume complexity budget without improving fit.

\subsection{Comparison with KAN}

KAN consistently underperforms in symbolic extraction: on all five Feynman
equations and all seven real-world datasets, KANSym returns either trivial
constant or linear formulas ($R^2 \approx 0$) or excessively complex formulas
that fail to generalise.  The raw KAN (numeric only) performs comparably to
other black-box models on most regression tasks but provides no interpretable
output and fails entirely on classification tasks (heart disease: F1 = 0.0).

This suggests that KAN's symbolic extraction step is brittle under
intercorrelated inputs, categorical variables, and moderate dataset sizes.
SR avoids this fragility by constructing interpretable expressions by design
throughout the search.

\subsection{Feature Discovery Without Engineering}

A noteworthy finding across datasets is that DVPS identifies features that
conventional feature selection methods overlook.  In the RAINE BMI dataset
(125 features), standard variable-importance methods (based on Random Forest
or XGBoost SHAP values) consistently rank anthropometric measurements
(prepreg\_weight, birth weight, BMI z-scores) as dominant—correctly.
However, they miss sparse but informative binary features such as maternal
asthma history and parental separation indicators, which appear in fewer than
5\% of records and are therefore down-weighted by impurity-based importance.
DVPS, operating on genetic algorithm selection pressure rather than marginal
importance, retains these rare features when they contribute to formula
accuracy.  The discovered longitudinal BMI formula explicitly includes a
polygenic risk score and early childhood BMI z-score, features that survive
pruning because of their robust information content despite being sparse in
the dataset.

\subsection{Limitations}

\textbf{Computational cost.}  SR is significantly more expensive than
gradient-based methods.  Each of our 27 grid configurations requires
500 iterations of evolutionary search across 100 populations.  While
parallelisable on HPC infrastructure (approximately 1,200–2,500 CPU-hours
per dataset), this precludes use on personal hardware without reducing the
grid.

\textbf{Operator set sensitivity.}  The recovered formulas depend on the
choice of operators.  The gravitational force equation (I.9.18) was not
exactly recovered, likely because the correct form requires division by a
sum of squared differences—a structure that is hard to evolve without
explicit guidance.

\textbf{Multi-layer validation.}  The DeepSR multi-layer architecture
requires more iterations per dataset (each layer requires its own SR run)
and was evaluated fully only on the RAINE BMI and selected biomedical
datasets at the time of writing.

\textbf{High class imbalance.}  The stroke dataset demonstrates that
symbolic classification under severe class imbalance remains challenging.
While DeepPySR improves recall without SMOTE, further work on explicit
imbalance-aware SR loss functions is needed.

% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We introduced DeepPySR, an enhanced symbolic regression framework with three
contributions: a dynamic variable pruning schedule (DVPS) that automatically
eliminates irrelevant features during evolutionary search; an exponential
Pareto selection criterion (EPS) that principally selects the best formula
from the complexity–accuracy trade-off frontier with user-tunable
accuracy–simplicity priorities; and multi-layer symbolic regression (DeepSR)
for hierarchical equation discovery that naturally handles intercorrelated
predictor clusters.

On five Feynman physics benchmarks, DeepPySR recovers exact symbolic
expressions for multiple fundamental equations and achieves more compact
formulas than PySR on high-dimensional equations.  On seven real-world
datasets spanning biomedicine and education, DeepPySR outperforms vanilla
PySR and all black-box baselines on five tasks (body fat, heart disease,
red wine quality, student performance in Math and Portuguese), approaches
Random Forest on BMI longitudinal prediction (R$^2 = 0.762$ vs.\ 0.765),
and returns interpretable formulas containing clinically, physically, and
socially meaningful variables.  Crucially, KANSym fails on all datasets,
confirming that differentiable symbolic extraction cannot yet replace
evolutionary search for real-world symbolic discovery.

These results position DeepPySR as a practical tool for scientific discovery:
it bridges the gap between accuracy-oriented machine learning and the
equation-based knowledge sought by domain scientists.  Future work will extend
the multi-layer architecture to additional datasets, investigate uncertainty
quantification for the discovered equations, incorporate imbalance-aware
fitness functions for classification, and integrate dimensional analysis
constraints for physical science applications.
