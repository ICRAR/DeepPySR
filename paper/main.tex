\begin{abstract}
Symbolic regression (SR) discovers analytical equations from data, providing
glass-box models where the formula is the explanation—unlike black-box
approaches augmented with post-hoc attribution tools such as SHAP or LIME,
which are unstable and unfaithful to the underlying model.  This transparency
is essential in clinical medicine and social science, yet three challenges
limit SR's real-world applicability: high-dimensional feature spaces, principled
formula selection from Pareto fronts, and data irregularities including
multicollinearity and class imbalance.  We present \textbf{DeepPySR} with
three contributions: a dynamic variable pruning schedule (DVPS) that eliminates
irrelevant features during evolutionary search; an exponential Pareto selection
criterion (EPS) selecting formulas via a user-tunable accuracy–complexity score;
and a multi-layer architecture for hierarchical symbolic composition.  On five
Feynman physics benchmarks and seven biomedical and social-science datasets,
DeepPySR outperforms PySR and all black-box baselines on body fat
(R$^2$: 0.794 vs.\ 0.702), heart disease (F1: 0.898 vs.\ 0.787), and student
performance (R$^2$: 0.964 vs.\ 0.948), returning interpretable formulas
aligned with domain knowledge.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The tension between predictive accuracy and interpretability is a central
problem in applied machine learning.  In scientific disciplines—from biomedicine
to astrophysics—understanding \emph{why} a model makes a prediction is often
as important as the prediction itself.  This need is especially acute in
\emph{high-stakes} domains such as clinical medicine, public health, and social
science, where model decisions directly affect individuals and must be auditable,
legally defensible, and comprehensible to domain experts who are not
machine-learning practitioners~\citep{rudin2019stop}.  A cardiologist needs not
just a risk score but the mechanistic factors driving it; an epidemiologist
studying childhood BMI trajectories needs an equation that can be communicated
and acted on.

The prevailing response has been to train high-accuracy black-box models—deep
neural networks, gradient-boosted trees—and apply \emph{post-hoc} explanation
tools such as SHAP~\citep{lundberg2017unified} or LIME~\citep{ribeiro2016why}
to approximate local feature attributions.  This strategy has well-documented
shortcomings: post-hoc explanations are approximations of a separate unexplained
model and may be \emph{unfaithful}; both SHAP and LIME are \emph{unstable}—
small perturbations can produce qualitatively different attributions~\citep{alvarez2018robustness};
and explanation methods can be \emph{fooled}—a biased model can produce
fair-looking SHAP values by design~\citep{slack2020fooling}.  No amount of
attribution can substitute for knowing the actual function the model
computes~\citep{rudin2019stop}.

Symbolic regression (SR) offers a different paradigm: it directly searches for
an analytical, human-readable mathematical expression that fits the data.  The
result is a \emph{glass-box} model—the formula \emph{is} the explanation, not
an approximation of one~\citep{koza1994genetic,cranmer2023interpretable}.
This makes SR particularly well-suited to high-stakes applications where
practitioners need equations they can inspect, validate against domain theory,
and communicate to non-technical stakeholders.  The field has seen renewed
interest following the release of PySR~\citep{cranmer2023interpretable} and
the Feynman SR benchmark~\citep{udrescu2020ai}, with recent methods achieving
exact recovery of fundamental physics laws.  Kolmogorov-Arnold Networks
(KAN)~\citep{liu2024kan} have been proposed as a gradient-based interpretable
alternative; we show that KAN's symbolic extraction fails systematically on
real-world benchmarks and is further limited by its dependence on a
pre-specified architecture (number of layers and nodes), making it impractical
when the correct functional form is unknown—the typical setting in exploratory
science.

Real-world biomedical and social-science datasets impose additional difficulties
that existing SR methods do not fully address.  \emph{Intraclass correlation
and multicollinearity} (ICC) are ubiquitous: weight, waist circumference, and
BMI z-score are near-collinear, as are fasting glucose, HbA1c, and insulin.
Standard SR may collapse onto a cluster of correlated variables, saturating
complexity without improving generalisation.  \emph{High dimensionality}
($d \gg 10$) causes evolutionary search to spend excessive effort on irrelevant
features.  \emph{Class imbalance}—stroke events affect fewer than 5\% of
participants in population studies—degrades classification performance when
handled only by resampling.  \emph{Formula selection} from the Pareto front
of accuracy–complexity trade-offs remains largely heuristic.  Hierarchical
structure, common in biomedical systems, cannot be captured by a single-layer
$y = f(\mathbf{x})$ formulation.  Prior work has addressed feature selection
as a pre-processing step~\citep{hein2018interpretable,virgolin2020improving}
and explored multi-layer SR~\citep{kim2020integration}, but no framework
integrates these components with automated latent variable discovery in a
modern evolutionary engine.

We present \textbf{DeepPySR}, an extension of SymbolicRegression.jl~\citep{cranmer2023interpretable},
with three contributions.  (1)~\textbf{Dynamic Variable Pruning Schedule
(DVPS)}: a scheduled mutation operator that linearly ramps up pruning pressure
during evolutionary search, automatically eliminating irrelevant features
without a separate pre-processing step, and making the framework usable on
datasets with hundreds of variables.  (2)~\textbf{Exponential Pareto Selection
(EPS)}: a principled, user-tunable scoring function that selects the single best
formula from the Pareto front by jointly rewarding accuracy and penalising
complexity exponentially; the parameter grid search is computationally
negligible as it scores existing hall-of-fame equations rather than retraining
the model.  (3)~\textbf{Multi-layer symbolic regression}: a hierarchical
architecture that discovers latent intermediate symbolic functions, composing
them layer by layer to handle ICC and capture hierarchical biological structure.
We evaluate these contributions on five Feynman physics benchmarks and seven
real-world datasets spanning biomedicine and education, including the RAINE
Generation 2 longitudinal cohort.

DeepPySR and PySR both recover exact formulas ($R^2 = 1.0000$) for Euclidean
distance (I.8.14: $[(x_1-x_2)^2+(y_1-y_2)^2]^{0.5}$) and kinetic energy
(I.13.4: $0.5m(v^2+u^2+w^2)$), matching the true physical laws.  For the

$R^2 = 0.9999$ with 37 nodes while PySR requires 44 nodes, illustrating that
DVPS produces more compact approximations through aggressive variable pruning.
KANSym fails on every equation ($R^2 = 0.0$ in four of five cases), and the
numeric KAN fails on equations requiring non-monotone structure.

\textbf{Body fat.}  DeepPySR achieves R$^2 = 0.794$, improving over PySR by
$+0.092$ (13\% relative) and over all black-box baselines.  The margin over
ElasticNet ($+0.101$) is particularly noteworthy: a linear combination of
correlated anthropometrics captures substantial variance, but the non-linear
interaction terms discovered by DVPS—a sinusoidal weight correction and a
Neck–Ankle cross-term—capture an additional 10 percentage points that linear
models cannot represent.  Without pruning, PySR includes redundant correlated
features (chest, bicep, forearm) that consume complexity budget without
predictive gain.  KAN and KANSym collapse entirely ($R^2 = 0.0$) on this
highly intercorrelated dataset.  The interpretable formula ($R^2 = 0.776$,
41 nodes) retains 97.7\% of full accuracy: $-\text{Wri} + \text{Abd}
- 51.61/\sin(224.74/W) + \sin(0.79\,\text{Nck}) + \ldots$, centred on
abdomen circumference—the primary variable in established clinical body fat
equations (Durnin–Womersley), lending face validity.

\textbf{Wine quality.}  DeepPySR achieves R$^2 = 0.412$ on red wine,
outperforming PySR by $+0.104$ (34\% relative, the largest proportional gain
across all regression datasets) and matching Random Forest (0.405).  DVPS's
variable pruning is particularly beneficial here: volatile acidity, alcohol,
and sulphates are the true dominant predictors, but correlated features
(density, pH) mislead a non-pruning search.  The interpretable red wine formula
($-\text{VA} - \text{Cl} + \sqrt{\text{alc}\cdot(-0.2\,\text{pH}/\text{sul}+4.8)}$;
$R^2 = 0.366$, 25 nodes) is chemically interpretable: volatile acidity
negatively correlates with quality, chlorides proxy bitterness, and the
alcohol–pH–sulphate interaction captures fermentation balance.  White wine is
harder due to noisy subjective ratings; MLP achieves the best overall
$R^2 = 0.457$ while DeepPySR (0.349) produces the only interpretable
closed-form formula.

\textbf{Student performance.}  DeepPySR achieves R$^2 = 0.964$ (Math) and
$R^2 = 0.924$ (Portuguese), substantially outperforming all baselines.  The
advantage over tree ensembles is striking: Random Forest achieves $R^2 = 0.865$
(Math) and 0.836 (Portuguese), gaps of $+0.099$ and $+0.088$.  Tree ensembles
use all three grade variables (G1, G2, G3) as features and suffer from
multicollinearity, whereas DeepPySR's multi-layer architecture selects G2 as
the primary anchor and uses G1 and social variables as secondary adjustments in
inner layers, producing a coherent formula: $\mathrm{piecewise}(\text{nursery}
+ \text{famrel} - 5.39\,\mathrm{piecewise}(\ldots,\text{Walc}) + \sin(h),\,G2)$.
The piecewise operator captures the fact that students in specific social
contexts (high absences, high alcohol, low family relations) show divergent
grade trajectories—a pattern aligned with educational research on social risk
factors.

\textbf{Heart disease.}  DeepPySR achieves F1 = 0.898, outperforming PySR
(0.787) by $+0.111$ (14\% relative) and Extra Trees (0.805), the best
black-box baseline, by $+0.093$.  Precision of 0.930 is the highest across
all models, meaning DeepPySR's positive predictions are highly reliable—
critical in a clinical screening context where false positives carry a cost.
The discovered formula involves oldpeak, restecg, trestbps, sex, ca, exang,
chest pain type, and thal—all established coronary risk factors—suggesting the
symbolic search has recovered genuine clinical decision logic.  LogisticRegression
(F1 = 0.821) is the next strongest interpretable model, confirming linear
separation captures much of the signal, but the piecewise operator in the
DeepPySR formula captures non-linear risk stratification that a linear
classifier cannot represent.  KAN and KANSym collapse to the majority class
(F1 = 0.0).

\textbf{Stroke.}  PySR optimises accuracy, achieving 93.6\% by predicting the
majority class almost exclusively (F1 = 0.082, recall = 0.067)—a degenerate
solution with no clinical utility.  DeepPySR achieves recall = 0.550 and
F1 = 0.257 at 86.5\% accuracy, identifying over half of stroke cases without
SMOTE augmentation.  The piecewise operator creates natural decision boundaries
that adapt to the minority class, performing implicit re-weighting through
formula structure rather than artificial oversampling.  Logistic regression and
tree ensembles with SMOTE achieve higher recall (0.656–0.770) at the cost of
lower accuracy and require explicit imbalance handling; DeepPySR achieves
comparable recall natively.

DeepPySR ($R^2 = 0.762$) approaches the best black-box model (Random Forest:
0.765) and substantially outperforms PySR ($+0.097$) and all other baselines.
The interpretable DeepPySR model (29 nodes; $R^2 = 0.606$) remains competitive
with XGBoost (0.603) while returning a human-readable formula.
This 125-feature, severely missing dataset with high ICC exemplifies settings
where DVPS is most valuable: standard variable-importance methods (Random
Forest, XGBoost SHAP) consistently rank anthropometric measurements as
dominant—correctly—but miss sparse yet informative features such as polygenic
risk scores, which appear for fewer than all participants and are down-weighted
by impurity-based importance.  DVPS, operating on evolutionary selection
pressure, retains these features when they contribute to formula accuracy.

The \textbf{interpretable formula} (29 nodes; $R^2 = 0.606$), selected by EPS
with $r^2$-weight~$= 1.0$ and $\lambda = 0.005$:
\[
  \hat{y} = z_5^{\mathrm{BMI}} + \Bigl(\mathrm{PRS}_{\mathrm{BMI}} +
             \frac{0.74\,e^{z_5^{\mathrm{BMI}} + (\mathrm{PRS}_{\mathrm{BMI}} + z_1^{\mathrm{BMI}})
             (-\mathrm{asthma}_m + \mathrm{death}_p)}}{\mathrm{mht\_wt}}
             + 8.37\Bigr)\log(\mathrm{age}),
\]
where $\mathrm{PRS}_{\mathrm{BMI}}$ is the polygenic risk score (PGS002313),
$z_5^{\mathrm{BMI}}$ and $z_1^{\mathrm{BMI}}$ are BMI z-scores at ages 5 and 1,
and $\mathrm{asthma}_m$, $\mathrm{death}_p$, $\mathrm{mht\_wt}$ are maternal
asthma, paternal bereavement, and maternal height-weight ratio.
The formula is biologically meaningful: adult BMI is governed by early
childhood growth and genetic risk ($\mathrm{PRS}_{\mathrm{BMI}}$), scaled
logarithmically with prediction age, and modulated by perinatal adversity
terms~\citep{warrington2019maternal}.

to PRS and early childhood BMI z-score, while sociodemographic variables appear
in outer layers—directly visualising how the multi-layer architecture decomposes
ICC clusters.  In the student performance plot (Fig.~\ref{fig:circle}a), G2
is selected via a thick Layer-1 arc as the dominant predictor of G3, with
social variables appearing in Layer 2.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

DeepPySR demonstrates that evolutionary symbolic regression, augmented with
principled feature pruning and formula selection, can match or exceed black-box
machine learning on real-world biomedical and social-science datasets while
returning human-readable equations.  This matters because the alternative—
fitting a black-box model and applying post-hoc attribution—produces
approximations of approximations: SHAP values explain a model, not the
underlying phenomenon, and they can be unstable or deliberately
manipulated~\citep{slack2020fooling,alvarez2018robustness,rudin2019stop}.
A discovered symbolic formula, by contrast, is directly inspectable, can be
validated against domain knowledge, and can be applied without software
dependency—properties that are not achievable through post-hoc explanation of
any black-box model.

The three contributions address distinct failure modes of existing SR.  DVPS
resolves the curse of dimensionality by embedding adaptive feature elimination
within the evolutionary loop rather than as a separate pre-processing step,
which would require knowing in advance which features are relevant.  Across
6 of 7 real-world datasets, DVPS configurations outperform the no-pruning
PySR baseline; the benefit scales with dimensionality, from negligible on
4-variable physics equations to a $13\times$ MSE reduction on the 125-feature
RAINE BMI task.  Crucially, DVPS retains sparse but informative features
(polygenic risk scores) that impurity-based feature selection discards, because
evolutionary selection pressure operates on formula accuracy rather than
marginal predictor importance.  EPS resolves the formula selection problem: by
searching existing hall-of-fame equations with a user-tunable exponential
penalty, it consistently selects more generalisable formulas than naive
accuracy maximisation—reducing average complexity by 12–18 nodes without
sacrificing predictive performance.  The multi-layer architecture resolves ICC:
rather than collapsing onto an arbitrary linear combination of correlated
predictors, the outer SR layer selects a single representative variable and
inner layers reveal its functional dependence on correlated cluster members,
producing formulas that are both more compact and more interpretable.

The comparison with KAN is instructive.  KANSym fails on every benchmark—
producing constant or degenerate formulas on all five Feynman equations and
seven real-world datasets—while the numeric KAN performs comparably to other
black-box models but provides no interpretable output and collapses entirely on
classification tasks.  KAN's failure is not simply a matter of training budget:
the symbolic extraction step is brittle under multicollinearity, categorical
inputs, and the requirement to pre-specify the network architecture.  This
last limitation is particularly consequential for exploratory science, where
the correct functional form is unknown; evolutionary SR does not require such
architectural prior knowledge.

DeepPySR's glass-box output has direct practical implications.  The heart
disease formula recovers all eight established coronary risk factors
(oldpeak, restecg, trestbps, sex, ca, exang, chest pain type, thal) in an
auditable piecewise expression, enabling clinicians to understand and challenge
predictions.  The RAINE BMI formula—PRS times exponential early BMI z-score
scaled by log age—encodes a biologically plausible interaction between genetic
susceptibility and developmental trajectory that no post-hoc attribution method
could have produced, because it requires discovering the functional form, not
just ranking variables.  The wine quality formula (three-term expression
involving volatile acidity, chlorides, and an alcohol–pH–sulphate interaction)
is immediately actionable by a winemaker.

Several limitations remain.  SR is significantly more expensive than
gradient-based methods: the 27-configuration grid requires 1,200–2,500
CPU-hours per dataset and requires HPC infrastructure.  The recovered formulas
depend on the operator set; the gravitational force equation was not exactly
recovered, likely because the correct form requires division by a sum of
squared differences—a structure that is hard to evolve without guidance.
The multi-layer architecture's runtime scales with both the number of input
variables and the depth of the model, as each layer requires an independent
SR run.  Classification under severe class imbalance remains challenging:
while DeepPySR improves recall without SMOTE, explicit imbalance-aware fitness
functions would strengthen this further.  Future work will investigate
uncertainty quantification for discovered equations, incorporate dimensional
analysis constraints for physical science applications, and extend the
multi-layer architecture to broader classes of scientific datasets.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

\subsection{Symbolic Regression Preliminaries}
\label{sec:sr_prelim}

Let $\mathcal{D} = \{(\mathbf{x}_i, y_i)\}_{i=1}^N$ be a dataset with
$\mathbf{x}_i \in \mathbb{R}^d$ and $y_i \in \mathbb{R}$.  SR seeks an
expression tree $f$ drawn from a grammar $\mathcal{G}$ of operators
(e.g., $+, \times, \sin, \exp$) and operands (input variables or constants)
such that $f(\mathbf{x}) \approx y$.  The search space is combinatorially
large, so evolutionary algorithms evolve a population of candidate trees by
repeated mutation and selection.

We build on SymbolicRegression.jl (the Julia back-end of
PySR)~\citep{cranmer2023interpretable}, which runs multiple independent
evolutionary populations in parallel and periodically migrates the best
individuals across populations.  The cost of each expression combines
predictive loss $\mathcal{L}$ and a parsimony penalty proportional to
the number of nodes~$c$:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{cost}(f) = \mathcal{L}(f) + \beta \cdot c(f),
  \label{eq:base_cost}
\end{equation}
where $\beta$ is the parsimony coefficient.  After search, PySR returns a
Pareto front of non-dominated (loss, complexity) pairs.

\subsection{Dynamic Variable Pruning Schedule (DVPS)}
\label{sec:dvps}

In high-dimensional datasets ($d \gg 1$), expression trees tend to include
many irrelevant variables early in the search because any variable can reduce
loss slightly by overfitting.  Static parsimony penalises tree size but does
not specifically target variable diversity.  We introduce a dedicated mutation
operator, \texttt{prune\_variable}, that replaces a leaf variable node with a
numeric constant.  Its weight in the mutation schedule grows linearly as the
search matures.

At iteration $t$, the effective weight of the pruning mutation is:
\begin{equation}
  w_{\mathrm{prune}}(t) = w_0 \cdot \tau(t) \cdot M,
  \label{eq:prune_schedule}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
  \tau(t) = \min\!\left(1,\, \max\!\left(0,\,
            \frac{t - T_{\mathrm{start}}}{T_{\mathrm{ramp}}}\right)\right),
\end{equation}
where $w_0$ is the base pruning weight (default $1.0$), $T_{\mathrm{start}}$
is the iteration at which pruning begins, $T_{\mathrm{ramp}}$ is the ramp
duration over which the weight linearly increases from $0$ to full, and $M$ is
the maximum multiplier cap.  Before $T_{\mathrm{start}}$, $\tau(t) = 0$ and
no pruning occurs; after $T_{\mathrm{start}} + T_{\mathrm{ramp}}$, $\tau(t)
= 1$ and pruning is applied at full weight $w_0 \cdot M$.

This schedule has three desirable properties: (i) early iterations are
unaffected, allowing exploration of all features; (ii) pruning pressure ramps
smoothly, preventing sudden disruption of the population; (iii) a cap $M$
prevents pruning from overwhelming other mutation operators.  The schedule is
implemented in \texttt{Mutate.jl} of SymbolicRegression.jl.

Algorithm~\ref{alg:dvps} summarises the full evolutionary loop with DVPS.

\begin{algorithm}
\caption{Evolutionary SR with Dynamic Variable Pruning Schedule}
\label{alg:dvps}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\State \textbf{Input:} dataset $\mathcal{D}$, operators $\mathcal{G}$,
       params $T_{\mathrm{start}}, T_{\mathrm{ramp}}, M, w_0$
\State Initialise population $\mathcal{P}$ of random expression trees
\For{$t = 1, \ldots, T_{\mathrm{max}}$}
  \State $\tau \gets \min(1, \max(0, (t - T_{\mathrm{start}})/T_{\mathrm{ramp}}))$
  \State $w_{\mathrm{prune}} \gets w_0 \cdot \tau \cdot M$
  \State Update mutation weights with $w_{\mathrm{prune}}$
  \ForAll{individuals $f \in \mathcal{P}$}
    \State Sample mutation type from updated weight distribution
    \If{mutation = \texttt{prune\_variable}}
      \State Replace a random variable node with a constant
    \Else
      \State Apply standard mutation (e.g., \texttt{mutate\_operator})
    \EndIf
    \State Evaluate $\mathrm{cost}(f)$ on $\mathcal{D}$
    \State Update Pareto front
  \EndFor
  \State Migrate top individuals across sub-populations
\EndFor
\State \textbf{Return:} Pareto front of (loss, complexity) pairs
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Exponential Pareto Selection (EPS)}
\label{sec:eps}

After evolutionary search, SR produces a Pareto front $\mathcal{F} =
\{f_1, f_2, \ldots\}$ of non-dominated solutions indexed by accuracy and
complexity.  Selecting the best single formula is non-trivial: purely
accuracy-based selection favours the most complex equation; purely
complexity-based selection discards high-performing models.  We define the
following scoring function:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{score}(f_i) =
    \bigl[\max(10^{-4},\, R^2_i)\bigr]^{\rho}
    \cdot \exp\!\bigl(-\lambda\,(c_i - 1)\bigr),
  \label{eq:eps}
\end{equation}
where $R^2_i$ is the cross-validated R$^2$ of equation $f_i$, $c_i$ is its
node count, $\rho$ is a user-tunable accuracy exponent ($\rho \geq 1$), and
$\lambda > 0$ is a user-tunable complexity decay constant.  The selected
formula is $f^* = \arg\max_i \mathrm{score}(f_i)$.

The first factor rewards accuracy superlinearly (when $\rho > 1$), magnifying
differences between good and mediocre formulas.  The exponential decay penalises
complexity multiplicatively: each additional node reduces the score by
$e^{-\lambda}$, preventing complex equations from surviving with only marginally
higher R$^2$.  Both $\rho$ and $\lambda$ are user-tunable and grid-searched
via cross-validated performance.  Crucially, this grid search is computationally
negligible—it scores equations already stored in the evolutionary hall-of-fame
rather than re-running the model, so the entire sweep completes in seconds.
Users can supply domain-specific candidate pairs: a clinical audit requiring
compact formulas would set high $\lambda$, while an automated prediction task
might set low $\lambda$ and high $\rho$.  The floor $\max(10^{-4}, R^2_i)$
prevents numerical issues when $R^2 \leq 0$.

\subsection{Multi-Layer Symbolic Regression}
\label{sec:deepsr}

Many real-world systems exhibit hierarchical functional structure, and in the
presence of ICC, standard SR may converge to arbitrary combinations of mutually
predictive variables.  The multi-layer architecture addresses this by treating
correlated predictor clusters as subtrees: the outer function selects one
representative compound feature, and inner layers reveal how that feature is
constituted by correlated predictors.

The architecture discovers:
\begin{equation}
  y = f\!\bigl(g_1(\mathbf{x}),\, g_2(\mathbf{x}),\, \ldots,\, g_K(\mathbf{x})\bigr),
  \label{eq:deep_sr}
\end{equation}
where $f$ and each $g_k$ are symbolic expressions, and $K$ is the number of
latent variables.  Equation~\ref{eq:deep_sr} illustrates the two-layer case,
the primary architecture evaluated here; the recursion naturally generalises to
additional depths.  The algorithm uses a breadth-first queue: (1) run SR on the
original target against raw features to discover the outer expression $\hat{f}$;
(2) identify subexpressions in $\hat{f}$ as latent targets $\{g_k\}$; (3) run
SR for each $g_k$ with raw features as inputs; (4) substitute $\hat{g}_k$
back into $\hat{f}$; (5) repeat until no latent variables remain or maximum
depth is reached.  Each SR call uses DVPS and EPS.  The composed expression
is evaluated via SymPy for symbolic manipulation and \texttt{lambdify} for
vectorised prediction.  Runtime scales with the number of input variables
and model depth, as each layer requires an independent SR run.

\subsection{Datasets}
\label{sec:datasets}

\textbf{Feynman physics benchmarks.}  We use five equations from the Feynman SR
Benchmark~\citep{udrescu2020ai}: Gaussian distribution (I.6.2a; 1 variable),
Euclidean distance (I.8.14; 4 variables), kinetic energy (I.13.4; 4 variables),
gravitational force (I.9.18; 7 variables), and electromagnetic scattering
(I.32.17; 6 variables).  Each is evaluated on $N = 1{,}000$ noise-free samples
drawn from uniform random ranges.

\textbf{Body fat}~\citep{johnson1996fitting} (252 samples, 14 features;
regression).  Body fat percentage alongside 13 highly intercorrelated
anthropometric measurements.

\textbf{Heart disease}~\citep{detrano1989international} (303 samples, 13
features; binary classification).  Cleveland Heart Disease dataset with
clinical measurements; moderate class imbalance (54\% positive).

\textbf{Wine quality}~\citep{cortez2009modeling} (Red: 1,599; White: 4,898;
11 features; regression).  Physicochemical measurements with subjective quality
ratings (noisy labels); red and white modelled separately.

\textbf{Stroke} (5,110 samples, 10 features; binary classification).  Medical
history and demographic features with severely imbalanced binary target
(4.9\% positive).

\textbf{Student performance}~\citep{cortez2008student} (Math: 395; Portuguese:
649; 33 features; regression).  Demographic, family, and school attributes with
strong ICC among the three grade variables (G1, G2, G3).

\textbf{RAINE Generation 2 BMI longitudinal.}  A prospective cohort tracking
participants from birth in Western Australia.  Task: predict BMI across seven
time points (ages 8–27) from early childhood measurements spanning birth, year
1, and year 5 only—creating severe missingness.  Approximately 125 candidate
features including anthropometric measures, polygenic risk scores
(SUM\_PGS002313, SUM\_PGS002853, SUM\_PGS002161), and sociodemographic
variables.  High ICC among predictors.

\textbf{Diabetes}~\citep{cdc2015brfss} (70,692 samples, 21 features;
binary classification).  CDC BRFSS self-reported health indicators with
severe class imbalance.  Due to reduced iteration budget, results are
reported in the Supplementary Material.

No feature engineering is applied to any dataset; all models receive raw
(label-encoded or standardised) features.

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

All experiments use 5-fold cross-validation with stratified splits for
classification tasks and covariate-stratified splits for regression.  For
class-imbalanced datasets, SMOTE oversampling is applied to baseline classifiers
for fair comparison; SR models are tested \emph{without} SMOTE to evaluate
native imbalance tolerance.

We grid-search 27 SR configurations: pruning parameters
($T_{\mathrm{start}} \in \{25, 50, 75\}$, $T_{\mathrm{ramp}} \in \{50,
100, 150\}$) and adaptive parsimony scaling ($\alpha \in \{1.0, 10.0, 50.0\}$).
EPS hyperparameters ($\rho \in \{1.0, 1.5, 2.0\}$, $\lambda \in \{0.001,
0.005, 0.01\}$) are searched independently over the resulting Pareto fronts.
An interpretable formula is additionally defined as the best-scoring formula
with complexity $\leq 40$ nodes.  Following cross-validated training, we
conduct (a) multi-layer analysis on the full dataset with circle plot
visualisation; (b) Wilcoxon signed-rank tests comparing DeepPySR and PySR
performance distributions across folds; and (c) convergence analysis tracking
per-iteration R$^2$ or F1.

All SR runs use: binary operators $\{+, -, \times, \div, \mathrm{piecewise}\}$
and unary operators $\{\exp, \log, \sin, \sqrt{\,\cdot\,}\}$; maximum tree size
40; 100 populations of 200 individuals; 200 evolution cycles per iteration; 500
iterations (100 for stroke and diabetes); parsimony coefficient $\beta = 0.001$;
pruning cap $M = 0.7$.

Baselines: XGBoost~\citep{chen2016xgboost}, Random
Forest~\citep{breiman2001random}, Extra Trees~\citep{geurts2006extremely},
ElasticNet/LogisticRegression, MLP (two hidden layers, 128–64 units, ReLU,
Adam), KAN~\citep{liu2024kan}, KANSym (symbolic KAN extraction), and PySR
(vanilla, without DVPS, same operator set and iteration budget).

For regression: R$^2$, RMSE, MAE.  For classification: accuracy, precision,
recall, F1.  Formula complexity is the number of nodes in the expression tree.

\subsection{Computational Resources}
\label{sec:compute}

Experiments were conducted on the Setonix supercomputer at the Pawsey
Supercomputing Research Centre and the ICRAR computing cluster.  Each SR
configuration was run as an independent parallel job.  The 27-configuration
grid requires approximately 1,200–2,500 CPU-hours per dataset.
